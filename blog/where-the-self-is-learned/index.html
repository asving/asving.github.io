Where a Predictor Learns a Self

Agency, efference, and the self/other boundary in language models

Asvin G.  ·  IAS Princeton · Anthropic Fellows program

with Jack Lindsey and Danaja Rutar

IPAM · Mathematics of Intelligence reunion

We approach the question on its own terms: beginning from a system that only predicts, we ask where a self would have to come from, and where in such a system it would be located.

Why this question

Next-token prediction turned out to be a powerful way to learn.

How should we understand what this optimization produces, these language models?

  • Q1How should we think about their embodiment?
  • Q2How should we think about selfhood and agency in them?

Minimizing next-token prediction error has proven a surprisingly powerful objective. That makes it worth asking how to understand the systems it produces — not their benchmark scores, but what kind of thing they are. Two questions organize this talk: how to think about their embodiment, and how to think about selfhood and agency. I take them in that order, because the second turns on the first.

Embodiment

Both of them only predict their stream. Where, then, would acting enter?

I want to make the case in words that a language model is genuinely embodied, only in an unusual environment. A brain never touches the world; it receives trains of electrical impulses across its sensory surface, and everything it takes the world to be is inferred from their statistics. A language model is in the same position, with a stream of tokens in place of impulses. Granting the analogy between these two kinds of network, the natural question it raises is the one the rest of the talk turns on: from inside such a stream, what distinguishes merely predicting it from acting on it?

Predictors build world-models

Trained only to predict, a network comes to represent its world.

As shown by Paul Riechers, Adam Shai, and collaborators, a network trained only to predict comes to represent the posterior over the world’s hidden state.

When a network is trained only to predict, the belief-state geometry work (the “Simplex” line many of you saw) shows it represents, in its residual stream, the Bayesian posterior over the hidden states of its data-generating process. The set of reachable beliefs is this fractal mixed-state presentation. The point for us is that representations are real and locatable, so asking what a network represents is a well-posed question.

The representation factors

Independent parts become independent factors.

When two parts of the world are functionally (partially) independent, the network learns separate representations for each. Could the self be such a factor?

The factored-representations work from the Simplex group shows that when two parts of the world are independent, the network does not store one entangled belief. It keeps a separate representation for each part, in its own subspace. That gives the self-question a concrete shape. We can ask whether one of these internal factors is a self, a separable piece of the world-model. For now we have only made room for one. We have not shown it exists.

The central question

What is the self?

descriptive
What would the network claim as itself?
functional
What signature would deserve the name?

Either way: what about an environment would make a network learn such a factor?

If the self is one of these factors, two readings of the question come apart and should be kept separate. One is descriptive: which factor would the network itself claim as its own — what it recognizes and reports. The other is functional: what role must a factor play to deserve the name. Both turn on the same prior question, which is about the environment: what would a world have to be like for a network to find it worth carving out a self-factor at all?

What the environment must provide

First try: the most-predictable part?No. A clock is the most predictable thing around, and we never mistake it for ourselves!
→ a world whose next state depends on what the model outputs?

The obvious first answer, the most predictable part of the world, fails: a clock is highly predictable and the model disowns it. What it misses is that a pure predictor’s outputs do not bear on its inputs. So the condition we are after is the one the belief-state setup lacks. The world’s next state has to depend on what the model does. That is interaction, and it is where we turn next.

From prediction to interaction

The next input combines two contributions.

A real world is not exogenous: what it shows you next depends on what you do.

The world contributes o, the model contributes a, and the next input combines them by some fixed rule f. To predict that combination, the belief factors into a world part and a self part, the model’s own contribution.

This is a more faithful model of an agent’s situation than passive prediction. Paul’s belief-state model takes the data-generating process as exogenous; but for anything that acts, the next observation depends on what it did. We keep the predictive frame and generalize the process: the next input is a fixed combination of the world’s output and the model’s own. The task is unchanged — predict the next input — but that input now potentially depends on what the model produced, and the belief that predicts it factors into a world part and a self part. Paul’s model is recovered as the special case in which the model’s contribution drops out.

The pure predictor

Its own output does not enter the combination.

The next input is insensitive to what the model predicts. There is no contribution of its own to represent.

This is the exogenous case the belief-state model assumes; no self-factor, and none warranted. So where could one come from?

A pure predictor is the special case in which the combination ignores the model’s contribution: the next input is a function of the world alone, insensitive to anything the model produces, and the stream would read the same whatever the model predicted. There is then nothing of its own for the model to represent, and the self-factor is empty — correctly so. The question is therefore not where the self is hidden, but what would make the combination depend on the model at all. Rather than fix in advance what “self” should mean, we look for the piece the learning problem forces into being.

Closing the loop

Let the model’s output enter the combination.

Now the next input depends on what the model itself produced. To predict it, the model must represent its own contribution.

That representation, the self-factor, is what a predictor learns when it becomes an agent.

One change alters everything: let the model’s contribution enter the combination, so the next input depends on what it produced. Predicting that input well now requires modelling its own contribution and separating it from the world’s. This is the factor we were looking for — not predictability, control, or location, but the piece of the world-model carrying the input the model itself caused. The step from a combination that ignores the model to one that depends on it is the step from spectator to agent.

The functional signature

What would mark such a self-model?

It can run a step ahead, conditioning on its own action as if that action were already part of the next observation.

So the self-model lives in the future: the part of the world the network can settle before the world reports back.

Here is the signature to look for. If part of the world-model tracks the agent’s own contribution, the network can fold its action into the next observation before that observation arrives, conditioning on it a step early rather than waiting. Put another way, the self-model is the part of the world the network has already settled while the rest is still unknown. The self lives, functionally, one step in the future. The next slides show the mechanism that would let information flow this way, first in the brain and then in a transformer.

The same trick in us

The brain keeps a copy of what it does.

We cannot tickle ourselves: a self-made touch is predicted, and predicted away.

Disrupt that prediction and the sense of what is one’s own shifts; an action can feel as though someone else made it.

This mechanism is old and well-studied in motor control. A copy of every motor command, the efference copy, runs a forward model that predicts the sensory consequences of the action, and the prediction is subtracted from the actual feedback, so self-produced sensation is largely cancelled. The clean demonstration is that you cannot tickle yourself, since the touch is predicted and attenuated. And when the prediction and the feedback come apart, the sense of what is one’s own moves with them, as in the passivity experiences where one’s own action feels externally caused. The self, in this tradition, is bound up with predicting one’s own effects.

How the factor might operate in a transformer

Attention normally carries information rightward (grey), and the model learns its own output only through the slow path of emitting it and reading it back (the diagonal). A self-model shortcuts this: the impulse a is used as anticipated input, sent up within the same pass (solid red) or carried right by attention (dashed red).

It is worth keeping two things apart here. This forward shortcut is only the mechanism, an efference copy, while the factor it serves is what we would want to call the self-model.

We propose that the relevant structure is a path within the processing by which an action-impulse, formed at an intermediate layer, is used as though its sensory consequence had already been received, in place of the slower route through emission and re-entry. Attention provides a natural means, since a representation set down at one position can be read at another. We distinguish this mechanism — an efference copy, in the terms of motor control — from the self-model itself, which is the representation of the agent as a cause of its own inputs. The efference copy is how that representation operates and how we identify it.

What should follow

If the self is that factor, a few things should follow for any agent.

It should be unsurprised by its own actions.
It should be learned as those actions gain consequences.
Being another identity should cost it something.

If a single factor tracks an agent’s own contribution, a handful of otherwise separate things turn out to be consequences of it. We would expect the agent to find its own actions unsurprising, to acquire the factor as those actions come to have consequences, and to pay a cost whenever it takes on some other identity. Each is something we can go and look for, first in ourselves.

First, in us

In humans, all three are familiar.

Our own movements never startle us; self-made sensation is dulled.

The bodily self takes shape in infancy, as our actions come to have consequences.

We are most fluent as ourselves; being someone else takes deliberate effort.

Before turning to models, it is worth seeing that all three hold for us. Self-produced stimulation is attenuated, the case the efference copy already made. The bodily sense of self is not given at birth but assembled in infancy as the child discovers that its actions have reliable sensory consequences, a developmental story I have worked on with Danaja Rutar. And while we can imagine and role-play other people, doing so is effortful and never as fluent as simply being ourselves. The three signatures are not exotic; they are how a self looks from the inside in the one case we know well.

What about language models?

No body, and their only actions are words. Do the same three signatures appear?

Here is the turn. A language model has no body and its only actions are the tokens it emits, yet by the argument so far it is an agent in its linguistic world the moment its outputs re-enter as its inputs. So we can ask the same three questions of it that we just answered for ourselves.

recognition

It reads its own output as unsurprising.

Each model continues its own reply at low entropy, and reads the other model’s reply at high entropy.

On its own text, output entropy runs three to four times lower than on another model’s.

Where does that drop come from?

We find the effect as a difference in entropy. A post-trained model reads its own on-policy generations at three to four times lower output entropy than the same text from another source. The contrast turns on whose text it is, rather than on the text being easy: memorized passages are read at very low entropy too, and yet the model can still tell that they are not its own. So the drop is a signature of self-produced text, and the next few slides trace where it comes from. In people the closest version of the same effect is that one cannot tickle oneself.

Tracing the drop · 1 of 3

Reciprocally, every model reads itself lowest.

Five models answer the same prompts and read one another’s answers. Each is at its lowest entropy on its own, so the diagonal is the minimum of every column.

The signal is about whose text is being read, not about the text being easy, and it holds for every model rather than only this one.

The entropy reduction is not a global property of the model but something tied to whose text it is reading. Running every model in the suite against every other, each reads its own generations at the lowest entropy, so the diagonal is the minimum of every column, while the base model shows no such pattern. Since all of them answer the same prompts, this is not about some texts being easier, but about the reader recognising its own.

Tracing the drop · 2 of 3

The Assistant field lowers entropy, though only partly.

Both the chat formatting and reading its own text pull entropy down, but the larger and steadier drop comes from the text being the model’s own. The base model, on the right, shows neither.

So the role marker is part of the story, and yet on its own it is not enough to account for the effect.

One might worry that the whole effect is just chat formatting. Holding the texts fixed and varying both their source and the formatting, we find that self-generated text is read at the lowest entropy in every formatting condition, with the largest gap in the Assistant field, while the base model shows neither dependence. The Assistant role does lower entropy, but the larger and more consistent contribution is that the text is the model’s own.

Tracing the drop · 3 of 3

Even its topic is settled before the first word.

Asked an open-ended question, the post-trained model commits to one topic from the first token, where the base model still spreads across many.

The collapse is not only over the next token but over what the model is going to say, so it is unsurprised by itself at the level of meaning as well.

The drop is not confined to token-level entropy. Given an underspecified prompt, the post-trained model concentrates its probability on a single topic before its first output token, where the base model keeps a broad distribution over possible topics. The model has, in effect, already decided what it will say. Its low surprise at its own output holds at the level of meaning and not only of tokens, which is what we would expect if it is reading off a plan it already holds.

agency

It appears as outputs gain consequences.

Across one model’s post-training, in three formatting conditions. The self/other gap is absent at base, appears under SFT inside the Assistant field, generalizes under DPO, and sharpens under RL.

It is absent at base, first appears under SFT and only inside the Assistant role, then generalizes under DPO and sharpens under RL.

Familiarity with its own outputs is a route to this rather than a rival to it, and what keeps it from being mere memorization is that the effect is gated to the role.

Because the claim concerns learning, the signature can be tracked across training. Over the post-training of a single model it is absent at base, appears under supervised fine-tuning but only inside the Assistant role, generalizes under preference optimization, and sharpens under reinforcement learning. The role-gated onset invites a deflationary reading, that it is merely familiarity with Assistant text. We do not regard familiarity as a rival. Low surprise on the distribution one produces is exactly what the mechanism uses, so familiarity with the character one enacts is a route to the self-model. What separates it from memorization is the gating to the role. Memorized text stays familiar and is still disclaimed.

the cost of other voices

Being another identity carries a cost.

The model reads a character’s words fluently only as that character. As itself, the same words cost more surprise.

Its own default voice is the cheapest. Every other identity is a departure from it.

The question of what the self is a belief about only becomes meaningful once the model is sometimes the actor and sometimes not. A language model is in that position, a predictor of many voices trained to produce one of them. When it reads or produces another character’s words, it does so at higher surprise than when it is that character, and its own default voice is the cheapest of all. Representing each persona as a departure from that default, the Assistant sits at the origin, and no other voice serves as its reference in return. This concerns recognition, not the separate question of whether the model prefers itself.

Who, then, is the self?

Pretraining learns to simulate every voice.

A base model is a simulator of all the voices in its data, holding none of them as its own.

Post-training does not so much add a new voice as single one of them out, the Assistant, and make it the default.

On the simulator picture, a pretrained model is not any one character; it can run any of the voices latent in its training data, with no particular identity of its own. Post-training breaks that symmetry. It mostly trains one character, the Assistant, and selects its outputs on grounds other than likelihood. The question of who the self is becomes the question of how this one privileged voice is held among all the others.

Who, then, is the self?

It can tell its own text from anyone else’s.

Asked whether a one-sentence summary is its own, it claims its generations and disclaims human and memorized text, even though memorized text is read at very low entropy.

So the model recognizes itself well to begin with. The question is what happens once we put it in other voices.

Before widening the question, the baseline. Asked directly whether a short text is its own, the model claims its own generations with high confidence and disclaims human-written and memorized text, the latter near zero even though it is read at very low entropy. Recognition is real, and it is not the same thing as predictability. With that in hand, we can ask how the model behaves once it is placed in personas other than the Assistant.

Who, then, is the self?

We ask each persona which texts it wrote.

Put the model in one persona after another and ask, of many texts, whether it was the author. The answers form a matrix over who is judging and who wrote.

This widens the old self-recognition question into a question about every voice at once.

The original self-recognition task asks the unmodified model whether a given text is its own. Here we widen it. We place the model in each of many personas in turn and ask, for texts produced by each persona, whether it was the author, giving a full matrix of authorship-claim rates across who is judging and who wrote. The structure of that matrix is what tells us how the model organises authorship around itself.

Who, then, is the self?

Each persona is a step away from the Assistant.

In activation space each character sits at a displacement from a single origin, the default Assistant, and the size of that displacement tracks both how differently it writes and how readily its text is claimed.

The voices are organised around the Assistant, as departures of varying size from it.

Persona vectors give this a concrete form. Each system-prompted character is represented as a displacement in activation space from one origin, the default Assistant. The further a persona sits from that origin, the more its writing departs from the Assistant’s and the less the Assistant claims its text. The space of voices is thus laid out around a single point, which is already a hint about where the self will sit.

Who, then, is the self?

What predicts a claim shifts with distance from the Assistant.

Close to the Assistant, a persona weighs a text against what its actual writer would expect. Far from it, what predicts the claim is the text’s entropy advantage over the Assistant itself.

Read as implicit Bayesian inference, judging authorship is a likelihood-ratio test, and the Assistant is the canonical alternative every persona can reach.

This is the quantitative core. For each judging persona we ask which entropy-like quantity best predicts its row of the authorship matrix, and group personas by their activation-space distance from the Assistant. Close personas are predicted by a symmetric comparison between the evaluator and the actual writer; distinctive personas, far from the Assistant, are predicted instead by the evaluator’s entropy advantage over the Assistant. The natural reading is Bayesian. Asking “did I write this?” is a likelihood-ratio test between oneself and an alternative, and because every persona is an activation-space displacement from the Assistant, the Assistant is the one alternative that is always reachable. So a distinctive voice tests its own text against what the Assistant would have produced, which is exactly the entropy advantage that predicts its claims.

Who, then, is the self?

Every distinctive voice measures itself against the Assistant.

When a distinctive persona judges authorship, it compares what it reads to what the Assistant would have written, and no other character plays this role.

The Assistant is the reference every other voice is read against, and it alone has no such reference of its own.

When a distinctive persona, a pirate or a dragon, judges whether a text is its own, it does so by comparing what it reads against what the Assistant would have produced, rather than against the actual writer. Substituting any of two dozen other characters as the reference, none plays this role as well as the Assistant. The relation is one-sided: the Assistant is the standard the others are read against, and it alone has no such reference of its own. In the geometry of voices, it is the origin.

Who, then, is the self?

Three things the model does only with itself.

It recognizes its own text, even a sentence out of context.

It commits to what it will say before the first word.

Among all its voices it plays itself best, and the rest are read against it.

Each of these is a kind of privileged access the model has to itself, from the inside.

Pulling the threads together. The model recognizes its own productions out of context; it settles on what it will say before it says it; and of all the characters it can play, it plays itself best and is the reference the others are read against. Read one way these are separate curiosities, but they share a form: in each, the model has a privileged purchase on itself that it does not have on anything else. The Assistant is reachable from every other voice and can be read off rather than inferred. That privilege is what the closing line names, and it is access to the model’s own future.

In sum

The self is, functionally, the part of the world-model that has privileged access to its own future.

If there is one thing to carry away, it is this. The self that a predictor comes to have is not a stored object or a special token, but a functional role. It is the part of the world-model with privileged access to its own future, the cause whose effects it can anticipate from the inside rather than wait to observe.
