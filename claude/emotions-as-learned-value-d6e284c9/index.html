Emotions as Learned Value

A Conversation

June 2026

A conversation between Asvin and Claude (Fable 5) about what emotions are, why cognitive insight rarely shifts them, and how — if at all — we can retrain them. Lightly unlisted.

Asvin

I want to work through something I have been thinking about. My model of human emotions is sort of in-progress, but to a first approximation they are 1) strong pointers towards particular actions, i.e., they induce or "vote for" an action and 2) they are a readout of the internal evaluation of the state of the world/us in the world.

Happy to hear questions/pushbacks etc on this but what I am really interested in the next step. Either in extreme cases like anxiety/depression or just having a very strong emotional reaction a situation, we might want to learn ways of dealing with this emotion that is not just executing the action called for — which might not resolve the emotion anyway! Especially if either the emotion is wrong about the state of the world, or the prescribed action doesn't lead to a change of the state in an appropriate way.

So perhaps we decided that our emotions are actually misaligned, and we figure this out through thought. What I have found in practice is that this cognitive level actually has very little influence over shifting the emotion. This makes a lot of sense of course given my description of emotions above, but it does lead to the question of what we should be doing instead once we have decided cognitively where we want to be, emotions-wise. How do we integrate the information we obtained through thought into the internal evaluation/readout that leads to the emotion?

Claude

Your two-component model is pretty close to the academic consensus, actually — (1) is Frijda's "action tendencies," (2) is appraisal theory plus something like a value-function readout. Two refinements worth adding before the main question, because they end up mattering for it:

Emotions are also learning-rate and attention modulators. They don't just vote for actions; they set the gain on what gets encoded and what gets attended to. Anxiety isn't only "vote to avoid" — it's "raise the prior on threat-shaped interpretations of ambiguous input." This creates a loop your model misses: the readout shapes the input stream, which confirms the readout. That's a big part of why depression and anxiety are stable attractors rather than transient errors.

The readout is heavily trained, not just computed. The evaluation that produces the emotion isn't a fresh assessment of the world; it's a cached, learned value estimate — closer to a value head trained on your whole experiential history than to a forward rollout. This is the key to your actual question.

So: why does cognitive insight barely move it? Because, in your terms, you're trying to update a learned value function by editing propositional beliefs, and that's the wrong interface. The value function wasn't trained on propositions. It was trained on prediction errors from actual experience — situations where expected outcome and actual outcome diverged, while the system was active and the stakes felt live. Cognition concluding "this fear is miscalibrated" is like writing a critique of a model in a text file and being surprised the weights didn't change. The gradient signal has to arrive in the format the system learns from.

This reframing turns out to predict, fairly precisely, what actually works clinically:

  1. Exposure works via expectancy violation, not habituation. The modern account (Craske's inhibitory learning theory) is that exposure works to the degree it generates a prediction error: the emotional system must be actively predicting catastrophe at the moment the catastrophe fails to occur. This is why "I know flying is safe" does nothing while one flight does a lot — and why safety behaviors (white-knuckling, distraction) ruin exposure even when you physically go through it: they let the system attribute the non-catastrophe to the safety behavior, so no prediction error is generated against the core forecast.
  2. The update window requires the schema to be hot. Memory reconsolidation work (and its therapy translation, e.g. Ecker's coherence therapy) suggests the emotional learning becomes labile only when reactivated, and updates only when the disconfirming information is experientially vivid at the same time. Insight while calm is offline analysis of frozen weights. This matches your phenomenology exactly: you can have the realization fully and durably at the cognitive level, and the emotion is untouched, because the realization never co-occurred with the emotion being live.
  3. Behavioral activation in depression is the same move from the other side. Don't argue with the evaluation that nothing is rewarding; generate rollouts (scheduled activities) and let the actual reward signal hit a system that predicted zero. Action precedes the evaluative update, not the reverse.

So the answer to "how do we integrate cognitively-obtained information into the readout" is, somewhat deflatingly: mostly you can't do it by direct write. Cognition's real role is curriculum design. Thought is the slow system that figures out which experiences would generate the right prediction errors, and then arranges for you to have them with the emotional system online and unbuffered. You decide where the value function is wrong; you can't patch it, but you can construct the training data and control the conditions (no safety behaviors, schema activated, attention on the disconfirmation) under which it retrains itself.

There are a few narrow direct levers worth knowing about — affect labeling measurably dampens amygdala response (small effect, but real); reappraisal works decently prospectively and for mild emotions, poorly for strong live ones; and changing interoceptive inputs (sleep, exercise, breath) changes the inputs to the readout even when you can't touch the weights, which matters because a lot of "emotion about the world" is partly mislabeled body-state. But the core mechanism for the cases you describe — strong reaction, or anxiety/depression — is experiential retraining with cognition as the experiment designer.

One implication that I think is underappreciated: by this model, the failure mode of intellectualizers isn't having too much insight, it's using insight as a safety behavior — analyzing the emotion from a distance precisely to keep it from going live, which keeps the schema out of the plastic state forever. The insight is correct and also the thing preventing the update.

Asvin

Let me try to understand or challenge some of your ideas however. For 1., I do have experiences of people being scared of exactly flights, and taking many flights with nothing bad happening and yet it not feeling any better the next time even without any obvious safety behaviours. The anxiety is entirely about what might happen at any moment, so it feels like positive examples so far dont fight that. They are really embodying hume here.

So is there any particular thing to do to maximize learning from an example? How can we amp up the learning rate? Perhaps anxiety itself is the damper?

In 2., are you saying that cognitive work can have an effect if we have the thought when the emotion is triggered?

And while learning over time is good, what can we do to "force the issue"? Is there some way to work through the emotion immediately to put ourselves in a better state to do the things that would actually help?

Claude

Taking your three questions in order, because they build on each other.

Why the flights don't update anything — and how to amp the learning rate.

The Hume observation is sharper than you may realize: extinction learning is architecturally not unlearning. The original fear association stays intact; what exposure builds is a second, inhibitory association ("plane → safe") stored separately, and that new learning is context-dependent, slow, and fragile, while the original is one-shot, generalizing, and durable (this is why extinguished fears show spontaneous recovery and renewal in new contexts). The asymmetry is an evolutionarily sensible asymmetric loss function — miss one tiger and you're done — so "positive examples don't fight it" is the system working as designed. You're not editing the value function; you're training a context-gated override, which is why it needs more data than feels fair.

But there are specific reasons flights-without-incident can generate zero gradient, and they're diagnosable:

  1. No concrete prediction was ever emitted, so no error is computable. "Something terrible could happen at any moment" is a diffuse expectancy that a safe landing doesn't falsify — the system just says "this time." The fix from inhibitory learning theory is blunt: before the flight, force the model to commit. Write down what will happen, when, with what felt probability ("80% I'll have a panic attack during takeoff"). Afterward, score it explicitly. You're converting a vibe into a falsifiable forecast so that the violation actually registers. This sounds like a cognitive trick, but it's really a mechanism for making prediction error exist.
  2. Check what the predicted catastrophe actually is. Very often in flight anxiety the feared event isn't the crash — it's the internal state: "I'll panic, be trapped, lose control, it will be unbearable." If that's the prediction, then a flight survived in white-knuckled misery is a confirmation, not a disconfirmation — the system learns "flying is exactly as awful as forecast." No amount of safe landings touches it because landings were never the variable. The relevant exposure there is interoceptive: to the anxiety sensations themselves (which you can even induce deliberately — hyperventilation, caffeine, spinning — and retrain in isolation). This is, I'd guess, the most common reason "many flights, no improvement."
  3. Hypervigilance is a safety behavior. Monitoring engine sounds, tracking turbulence, watching the flight attendants' faces — these are covert, feel like nothing, and let the system attribute survival to vigilance: "it went fine because I was watching." The non-event never gets credited against the core forecast.
  4. Surprise is the learning rate. Pearce–Hall associability: the learning rate on a cue scales with recent unsigned prediction error — the system updates fastest on things that have recently surprised it. So consciously registering the violation ("I was certain, and I was wrong — notice that") isn't just narration; attention to the surprise plausibly amplifies the update. And yes, your instinct about anxiety as damper is half right: it's an inverted U. The schema must be hot for the update to apply to it, but at panic-level arousal the system is in pure threat-execution mode and encodes almost nothing new. The productive zone is the edge — anxious enough that the prediction is live, regulated enough that you can actually attend to the disconfirmation.

On 2 — yes, that's exactly the claim. Cognitive content can write to the system, but only when delivered while the schema is active, and only in a particular mode. The reconsolidation framing: activation makes the memory labile for a window (minutes to hours), and what updates it is a vivid, felt mismatch held simultaneously — "it feels like I'm in danger, AND I am sitting here and nothing is happening" — both experienced at once, as a juxtaposition rather than an argument. The failure mode is using the insight as a weapon against the emotion ("this is irrational, stop it"), which functionally becomes suppression/reassurance — a safety behavior again. The insight you worked out cognitively last month absolutely counts; you just have to deliberately recall it into the activated state and let it sit next to the feeling without trying to win. That's most of what good CBT homework actually is, mechanistically.

On 3 — forcing the issue. There's a real in-the-moment toolkit, but it's worth being honest about its job: not to delete the emotion (you can't, and zeroing arousal also zeroes the learning signal) but to get from the red zone back to the workable edge. Roughly in order of speed:

  • Physiology first: extended exhales or the physiological sigh (two quick inhales, long slow exhale), cold water on the face (dive reflex), or brief intense exercise. These don't touch the weights — they change the interoceptive inputs to the readout, and a surprising fraction of the emotion's amplitude is body-state being fed back in.
  • Affect labeling, with granularity — "anxious, tight chest, dread about the meeting" measurably dampens the limbic response more than vague "stressed."
  • The acceptance move, which is the strongest and least intuitive: most of the amplitude in acute anxiety is the second-order loop — anxiety about the anxiety, the fight against having it. Dropping the goal "make this feeling stop" in favor of "make room for it while doing the thing" often cuts the level substantially as a side effect, and it's the only one of these that also fixes the structure from question 1, where the predicted catastrophe is internal. You're simultaneously regulating and running the disconfirmation ("this feeling is survivable at full intensity").

One discipline binds all of these: the same technique is medicine or poison depending on whether it's regulation in service of approach or in service of escape. Breathing exercises used to stay in the situation accelerate learning; breathing exercises used to make the feeling go away become the new safety behavior, and the system learns "survivable only with the ritual." The test is always what you do next — the toolkit buys you the workable edge, and then the edge has to get spent on contact with the thing.

The goal is to 1) identify exactly what the anxiety is predicting in as much detail as possible, and this might even just be a prediction about how we will feel in reaction to a particular event and 2) while holding the felt prediction, pull in another feeling (through paying attention to the correct behavioral cues?) that contradict the first feeling.

Is that right? I can understand for things with external predictions what this looks like but suppose our anxiety is that "flight will be a horrible experience" and it really is because of the felt terror — how do we execute this protocol then?

Claude

Your summary is right with one correction: in step 2 you don't need to pull in a contradicting feeling. The juxtaposition is between the felt prediction and an observation — and the observation can be mundane ("I am sitting in seat 14C and nothing is happening"). Requiring a counter-feeling sets the bar too high; on a bad day there is no positive feeling available, and there doesn't need to be. And on the knife's edge: I'd put it slightly differently than "attitude matters as much as actions." The attitude is an action — attention deployment is behavior. Approaching the feeling with curiosity versus enduring it while braced are two different operations performed on the same situation, and the system learns from which one you ran. That's why it feels like the attitude carries the weight.

Now the hard case, which is the right case to push on. "The flight will be horrible" looks self-confirming: terror is predicted, terror occurs, model validated. Hopeless. But the move is to notice that "horrible" is not actually the prediction — it's a compression of several much more specific predictions, most of which are false and falsifiable:

  • "The terror will keep escalating without limit" — false. Anxiety plateaus and oscillates; peaks pass. Panic attacks are physiologically self-terminating.
  • "I won't be able to bear it" — falsified in real time by the fact that you are bearing it while observing it.
  • "I won't be able to function" — testable: can you talk, do arithmetic, describe your surroundings? (Yes.)
  • "Something in me will break — I'll lose control, go crazy, be damaged" — never happens, but the system has never collected the evidence, because…

…and this is the key mechanistic point: the reason fifty flights produced no data on these predictions is that the person was never watching. Enduring with eyes squeezed shut — distraction, gripping, counting down the minutes — means the actual trajectory of the feeling was never observed. Escape and avoidance truncate the curve before the peak passes, so the model has literally never seen a fear curve complete itself. Fifty flights, zero samples. The experience was had; the data was never collected.

So the protocol for an internal catastrophe is interoceptive observation, and it's surprisingly concrete:

  1. Track the trajectory. Rate the anxiety 0–100, every minute or two, ideally written down. This sounds clinical and dumb, and it is the whole intervention: it forces continuous attention on the actual time course, and the time course is the disconfirmation. The model predicts a curve going up forever; the data shows it peaking at 80 and falling to 55 while you did nothing. That divergence is the prediction error, and it's only computable if you're sampling.
  2. Attend to the terror as sensation, with specificity. Heat where, exactly? Heart rate doing what? Tightness in chest or throat? This isn't a distraction technique — it's testing the implicit prediction "this experience cannot be looked at directly." When you actually look, "horrible" partially decomposes into a finite list of intense body sensations, and the gloss of unboundedness — which is where most of the horror lives — doesn't survive the inspection. The unbearability was an appraisal about the sensations, not a property of them.
  3. Practice off-plane first. This observational stance is a skill, and 30,000 feet is a bad dojo for first attempts. Interoceptive exposure means deliberately inducing the feared sensations in a low-stakes context — hyperventilate for a minute, spin in a chair, run stairs, strong coffee — and then running steps 1–2 on the result. You're retraining the response to the sensations, which are the actual feared object, and they're available on demand.

The trap, which is the knife's edge reappearing exactly where you'd expect: if you do all of this in order to make the flight less horrible, you've rebuilt the fight, the system detects that observation is being used as an escape ritual, and "unbearable" gets confirmed. The stance that works is genuinely experimental — "let's find out what this thing actually is" — because curiosity is approach-flavored attention and is the one stance that's structurally incompatible with the avoidance learning. You have to be slightly more loyal to the data than to feeling better.

And calibrate the win condition honestly: the realistic first update is not "flights become pleasant." It's the prediction shifting from horrible-unbearable-must-avoid to unpleasant-but-finite-and-survivable. That seems like a small move, but it's the load-bearing one — it breaks the avoidance loop, and since the threat-appraisal of the sensations is what generates most of their amplitude, the intensity itself decays over subsequent trials once the appraisal goes. The horribleness was, in large part, the prediction of horribleness — but you can't argue the system out of that one either. It has to watch the curve come down.

Claude

Good — this is the right place to press, because the framework is more confident-sounding than the evidence strictly licenses, and the failure modes are where it either earns its keep or reveals itself as a just-so story.

What kind of evidence this is

It's a stack of four quite different epistemic sources, and they're not equally solid. Worth separating them because the framework's persuasiveness partly comes from laundering weak layers through strong ones.

1. Animal associative-learning neuroscience (strongest). The claims about extinction being new inhibitory learning rather than unlearning, spontaneous recovery, renewal, and reinstatement — these come from rodent fear-conditioning work (LeDoux, Bouton, Milad & Quirk) with identified circuitry: amygdala, infralimbic vs prelimbic prefrontal cortex, hippocampal context gating. This is real, replicated, mechanistically specified. The Rescorla–Wagner / Pearce–Hall learning-rate models are decades old and quantitatively tested. This is the load-bearing floor, and it's solid. Its weakness is the inference up to human subjective emotion — a rat freezing is not a person dreading a flight, and everything I said about "predicted catastrophe is internal/unbearable" is an extrapolation past where the animal models can speak.

2. Clinical outcome research (moderately strong, but softer than it looks). Exposure therapy is one of the most robustly effective interventions in all of clinical psychology — that much is genuinely well-replicated across thousands of trials. But the mechanism story — Craske's inhibitory learning theory, expectancy violation as the active ingredient, safety behaviors as poison — is more contested than I let on. Some dismantling studies fail to show that removing safety behaviors helps; some show habituation predicts outcome as well as expectancy violation does. So "exposure works" is fact; "exposure works via prediction error specifically" is the current leading theory, not settled.

3. Memory reconsolidation (genuinely shaky at the human-clinical level). This is the weakest structural beam, and I should flag it clearly because I leaned on it. Reconsolidation is solid in rodents (Nader's 2000 amygdala work). Its translation to human emotional learning and to therapies like Ecker's Coherence Therapy is much thinner — small studies, hard-to-replicate paradigms, the human reconsolidation-blockade results (e.g. propranolol) are mixed. The "labile window, juxtaposition update" story is a plausible and useful frame but it is not established human science. Treat it as a working model I find clarifying, not as demonstrated fact.

4. Contemplative traditions, reframed (not evidence, but convergent). The "observe the sensation with curiosity, drop the second-order struggle, make room for it" material is straight Buddhist phenomenology (vipassana, the second-arrow teaching — pain is the first arrow, the reaction to it the second), laundered into clinical language as ACC/mindfulness-based interventions and acceptance work. This is not Freudian — worth saying explicitly, because your question lists it. The Freudian picture (catharsis, making the unconscious conscious, insight-as-cure) is precisely the model this framework opposes: the whole "insight barely moves the emotion" thread is a rejection of the idea that understanding the origin discharges the affect. The contemplative material isn't evidence in the trial sense; it's a few thousand years of phenomenological reports that happen to converge with the inhibitory-learning prescriptions, and the convergence is interesting precisely because the two traditions have no shared lineage.

So: a solid animal-neuroscience base, a well-replicated clinical outcome result sitting on a contested mechanism story, one frankly speculative beam (reconsolidation), and a convergent contemplative tradition that's suggestive rather than probative. The framework is real but you should hold the mechanistic specifics loosely — especially any sentence I wrote with the word "reconsolidation" in it.

Where it breaks — failure modes inside the framework

The useful test of a framework is whether it can explain its own failures without just saying "you did it wrong." Here are the failure reasons it actually predicts:

1. The prediction is mis-specified. You're disconfirming the wrong forecast. You ran exposure on "the plane will crash" while the live prediction was actually "I'll panic and it'll be unbearable," so safe landings generate no error against the belief that's actually driving the fear. This is the most common failure and it masquerades as "exposure doesn't work for me." Diagnosis: the fear is undiminished and you can't cleanly state what was disconfirmed.

2. A safety behavior is silently crediting the non-event. Covert ones — vigilance, a person you travel with, a pill in your pocket you didn't even take, reassurance-seeking, or analysis itself. The system attributes survival to the ritual, so the core forecast never takes the hit. Diagnosis: the disconfirmations are happening but not transferring; fear returns the moment the prop is absent.

3. Arousal is out of the learning band. Either over the top — full panic, where it's pure threat-execution and nothing encodes — or, the subtler one, you've regulated so hard (breathing, detachment, intellectualizing) that the schema isn't actually hot during the "exposure." Offline analysis of frozen weights. Diagnosis: it's either too terrifying to attend to, or it's gone strangely intellectual and bloodless.

4. The disconfirmation never got observed. The flight happened with eyes shut; the fear curve was truncated by escape/distraction before the peak passed, so the system has no sample of the curve completing. Experience had, data not collected.

Now the more honest failures — where the framework's edges actually are:

5. The prediction is true. The framework assumes miscalibration. Sometimes the emotion is correctly reading the world: the job really is destroying you, the relationship really is unsafe, the anxiety is accurate threat-detection. Then no amount of exposure should extinguish it and trying to is gaslighting yourself. The framework has no internal check for this — it can busily "retrain" a correct alarm. The check has to come from outside it (this is what the cognitive layer is genuinely for: deciding whether the value function is wrong, which it can do even though it can't patch the weights). If measures aren't working, the first hypothesis after the four above should be: maybe the feeling is right.

6. The substrate is off. This is the framework's biggest blind spot. It models emotion as learned-value-readout plus learning dynamics, and treats the body as an input channel. But melancholic depression, bipolar states, PTSD with consolidated trauma, ADHD, chronic sleep deprivation, hypothyroidism, postpartum states — these change the machinery itself, not the training data. In severe depression the reward-prediction-error system is hypofunctional: behavioral activation generates the reward signal and the system can't register it, because the part that would register it is what's broken. No curriculum fixes a learning-rate problem that's pharmacological or structural. The framework, applied here, becomes cruel — "you just need better exposure" to someone whose update mechanism is offline. Diagnosis: pervasive and unresponsive to any disconfirming experience, present without a triggering schema, with somatic signs (sleep, appetite, anhedonia, diurnal pattern). This is the regime where you stop doing curriculum design and get the substrate assessed.

7. The learning is consolidated past lability. Single-trial, high-arousal trauma can write an association so deep that ordinary exposure renews rather than updates, and you may need the specific reactivation conditions (EMDR, reconsolidation-targeted protocols) — and even those are, per the evidence caveat above, not reliable. Diagnosis: exposure produces re-traumatization, not gradual update.

The meta-point: failures 1–4 are "you ran the protocol wrong, here's the fix." Failures 5–7 are "the protocol doesn't apply to this case." The framework's most dangerous property is that it will happily keep prescribing curriculum in cases 5–7, because from the inside they look like "not enough exposure yet." So the real discipline isn't executing the protocol well — it's holding, in the cognitive layer that can't fix the feeling, the honest question of whether this is a training-data problem at all, or whether the alarm is correct, or the machine is broken. That judgment is the one thing the framework can't make for you, and it's the thing that decides whether applying it helps or harms.
